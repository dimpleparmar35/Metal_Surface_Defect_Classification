\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Surface defect detection is an essential process in metallurgical manufacturing to guarantee material quality, structural integrity, and aesthetic standards. Traditional methods relying on manual visual inspection are highly inefficient, expensive, and prone to human error due to fatigue and rapid production speeds. This study proposes a highly accurate, automated deep learning framework for classifying hot-rolled steel surface defects. We developed a Custom Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained entirely from scratch and benchmarked it against a transfer learning approach leveraging the pre-trained MobileNetV2 architecture. Both models were rigorously trained, validated, and evaluated on the public NEU Metal Surface Defect Database, which contains 1,800 grayscale images distributed equally across six distinct defect categories (Crazing, Inclusion, Patches, Pitted Surface, Rolled-in Scale, and Scratches). To ensure robust evaluation and prevent data leakage, a programmatic, stratified 70/15/15 train/validation/test split was implemented alongside real-time data augmentation. The MobileNetV2 transfer learning model, utilizing a combination of frozen feature extraction and subsequent fine-tuning of the deepest convolutional blocks, achieved superior classification results with a testing accuracy of 98.15\%, compared to 92.59\% for the Custom CNN. Furthermore, Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) was implemented to provide spatial interpretability of the models' predictions. The Grad-CAM heatmaps successfully demonstrated that the neural network classifier accurately isolates and focuses on the physical coordinates of the defects on the steel strips rather than spurious background noise, providing transparency critical for real-world automated surface inspection (ASI) deployment.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background and Motivation}
In the modern metallurgical industry, the production of hot-rolled steel strips is a massive, high-speed continuous process. During the rolling process, various physical and chemical anomalies can manifest on the surface of the steel. These anomalies, categorized as defects, include crazing, inclusions, patches, pitted surfaces, rolled-in scales, and scratches. Such defects occur due to a variety of uncontrollable environmental and mechanical factors, including rolling roll wear, mechanical vibration, slab impurities, and uneven temperature distribution during cooling.

The presence of these defects significantly degrades the mechanical properties, corrosion resistance, and aesthetic appeal of the final metal product. Consequently, real-time quality control is a vital checkpoint for modern steel fabrication processes. Historically, automated surface inspection (ASI) systems relied heavily on manual visual inspection by trained human operators. However, human inspectors struggle to keep pace with rapid rolling speeds that can exceed 20 meters per second. Furthermore, psychological and physical fatigue leads to highly inconsistent error rates, resulting in both false positives (discarding good steel) and false negatives (shipping defective steel).

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The core problem is the need for an automated, highly accurate, and real-time defect classification system that does not rely on human intervention or brittle, handcrafted mathematical features. The system must be robust enough to handle varying lighting conditions, scale differences, and the inherent intraclass variation found in industrial environments.

\subsection{Research Questions}
This project seeks to address the following fundamental research questions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Can deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) effectively automate the classification of six distinct hot-rolled steel surface defects with an accuracy exceeding 90\%?
    \item How does a Custom CNN, designed and trained from scratch, perform in comparison to a state-of-the-art pre-trained transfer learning architecture (MobileNetV2)?
    \item Can Explainable AI (XAI) techniques, specifically Grad-CAM, be utilized to visually prove that the neural networks are making classification decisions based on the actual physical defects rather than arbitrary background noise?
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
Automated inspection of hot-rolled steel strip defects has a rich and evolving literature, heavily mirroring the broader advancements in the field of computer vision.

\subsection{Traditional Machine Learning Approaches}
Early automated work focused entirely on traditional texture analysis and handcrafted feature extraction. Song and Yan \cite{song2013noise} proposed Completed Local Binary Patterns (CLBP) to capture defect patterns. They demonstrated that while texture features could classify certain distinct defects, they are highly susceptible to high noise levels and variations in steel surface textures. Similarly, researchers utilized Gabor filters and Gray Level Co-occurrence Matrices (GLCM) to extract statistical features, which were then fed into Support Vector Machines (SVM) or Random Forest classifiers. While computationally inexpensive, these methods proved brittle under variations in illumination, scale shifts, and ambient factory noise.

\subsection{Deep Learning and CNNs}
The transition to deep learning addressed the limitations of handcrafted features. Luo et al. \cite{luo2020automated} surveyed visual defect detection methods, showing definitively that CNN architectures outperform traditional handcrafted feature extraction in both classification accuracy and robustness against surface noise. CNNs automatically learn hierarchical spatial features directly from raw pixels. Yi et al. \cite{yi2017end} implemented a custom sequential CNN for the NEU dataset, demonstrating that an end-to-end network could extract features and classify defects simultaneously.

\subsection{Transfer Learning Paradigms}
More recently, Transfer learning has become the standard methodology in applied computer vision. Pre-training a deep network on massive datasets like ImageNet allows the model to learn highly generalized features. For edge computing and real-time factory processing where inference speed is critical, Sandler et al. proposed MobileNetV2 \cite{sandler2018mobilenetv2}. MobileNetV2 utilizes inverted residual blocks and depthwise separable convolutions to achieve high accuracy with a fraction of the computational parameters of standard networks.

\section{Mathematical Foundations}
To fully understand the mechanics of the deep learning pipeline, several mathematical foundations of Convolutional Neural Networks and Explainable AI must be established.

\subsection{The Convolutional Operation}
The core building block of a CNN is the convolutional layer, which applies a series of learnable filters to the input image to generate feature maps. For a 2D input image $I$ and a 2D filter $K$ of size $m \times n$, the discrete convolution operation at location $(i, j)$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
(I * K)(i,j) = \sum_{m} \sum_{n} I(i+m, j+n) K(m,n)
\end{equation}
This operation captures spatial dependencies such as edges and textures, which are critical for identifying metallurgical defects.

\subsection{Batch Normalization}
Batch Normalization is applied after convolutions to stabilize and accelerate training by reducing internal covariate shift. For a mini-batch of activations $\mathcal{B} = \{x_{1}, \dots, x_{m}\}$, the normalized activation $\hat{x}_i$ is computed as:
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu_{\mathcal{B}}}{\sqrt{\sigma_{\mathcal{B}}^2 + \epsilon}}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{\mathcal{B}}$ and $\sigma_{\mathcal{B}}^2$ are the batch mean and variance, respectively, and $\epsilon$ is a small constant for numerical stability.

\subsection{Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM)}
Grad-CAM provides visual explanations for CNN decisions. To obtain the class-discriminative localization map $L_{Grad-CAM}^c \in \mathbb{R}^{u \times v}$ for a specific class $c$, the gradient of the score for class $c$, $y^c$, is computed with respect to the feature map activations $A^k$ of a convolutional layer. The neuron importance weights $\alpha_k^c$ are calculated using global average pooling:
\begin{equation}
\alpha_k^c = \frac{1}{Z} \sum_i \sum_j \frac{\partial y^c}{\partial A_{i,j}^k}
\end{equation}
where $Z$ is the number of pixels in the feature map. The final Grad-CAM heatmap is a weighted combination of forward activation maps, passed through a ReLU to retain only features with a positive influence on the class:
\begin{equation}
L_{Grad-CAM}^c = ReLU \left( \sum_k \alpha_k^c A^k \right)
\end{equation}

\section{Methodology}
The proposed framework consists of data ingestion, real-time data augmentation, convolutional model construction, and model interpretability.

\subsection{Preprocessing \& Data Splitting}
The preprocessing pipeline was designed to handle data programmatically:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Resizing}: All images are bilinearly interpolated to 224x224 pixels to match MobileNetV2 inputs.
    \item \textbf{Normalization}: Grayscale pixel values are normalized to $[0, 1]$.
    \item \textbf{Stratified Splitting}: The dataset is split into Training (70\%), Validation (15\%), and Test (15\%) sets.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Real-time Data Augmentation}
To mitigate overfitting on 1,800 images, Keras \texttt{ImageDataGenerator} applied random rotations ($\pm 20^{\circ}$), flips, width/height shifts (10\%), and brightness adjustments (0.8 to 1.2).

\subsection{Model Architectures}
We implemented two architectures for comparison.

\subsubsection{Custom CNN Design}
The Custom CNN consists of four primary blocks. Each block contains a $3\times3$ \texttt{Conv2D} layer, Batch Normalization, and $2\times2$ \texttt{MaxPooling2D}. Filter counts double sequentially (32 $\rightarrow$ 64 $\rightarrow$ 128 $\rightarrow$ 256). The classification head uses Dense layers (512, 256) with 50\% Dropout.

\subsubsection{MobileNetV2 Transfer Learning Design}
We utilized MobileNetV2 pre-trained on ImageNet. Training occurred in two phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Feature Extraction}: The base weights were frozen. A new classification head was trained for 15 epochs.
    \item \textbf{Fine-Tuning}: All layers from index 100 onwards were unfrozen and trained with a low learning rate ($1 \times 10^{-5}$) for an additional 15 epochs.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Experimental Setup}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Loss Function}: Categorical Crossentropy.
    \item \textbf{Optimizer}: Adam optimizer (Initial LR = 0.001).
    \item \textbf{Callbacks}: \texttt{EarlyStopping} (patience=10), \texttt{ReduceLROnPlateau}, and \texttt{ModelCheckpoint}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}
The superiority of Transfer Learning was on full display. Despite MobileNetV2 being originally trained on natural images, its low-level filters generalized exceptionally well to industrial steel textures. The Custom CNN also performed admirably, proving that a network trained from scratch on heavily augmented data can still achieve industrial viability.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
This architecture is strictly an Image Classifier. Future research must evolve this pipeline into an Object Detection framework (e.g., YOLOv8) to draw precise bounding boxes around multiple distinct defects in real-time.

\section{Conclusion}
This research successfully developed an automated deep learning system for hot-rolled steel surface defect classification. The fine-tuned MobileNetV2 model achieved an exceptional test accuracy of 98.15\%, vastly outperforming the Custom CNN's 92.59\%. Grad-CAM visualization successfully verified that the neural networks physically localize the correct defect anomalies, providing a robust, highly accurate computer vision solution ready for industrial quality control.
