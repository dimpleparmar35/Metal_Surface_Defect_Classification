01 · Context

The Problem Statement

🏭 Background & Motivation

In the metallurgical industry, surface defects on hot-rolled steel strips severely impact the mechanical strength, corrosion resistance, and visual appearance of the final product. Defects occur due to rolling roll wear, mechanical vibration, slab impurities, and uneven cooling temperatures.

Historically, quality control relied on manual visual inspection. Human inspectors cannot keep pace with rolling speeds exceeding 20 meters per second, and fatigue leads to inconsistent error rates, causing both false positives (discarding good steel) and false negatives (shipping defective steel).

This creates an urgent need for an automated, real-time, and highly accurate surface inspection system that does not rely on human operators or brittle, handcrafted mathematical feature descriptors.

⚠️

False Negatives

Defective steel escapes inspection and reaches customers, causing safety hazards and costly recalls.

🚫

False Positives

Good steel is incorrectly rejected, directly increasing material waste and production costs.

⚡

Speed Limitation

Manual inspection cannot keep pace with modern production lines, creating critical QA bottlenecks.

🔬 Research Questions

Q1

Can deep CNNs effectively automate classification of 6 distinct steel surface defects with accuracy exceeding 90%?

Q2

How does a Custom CNN trained from scratch compare to fine-tuned MobileNetV2 Transfer Learning in accuracy and training efficiency?

Q3

Can Grad-CAM heatmaps visually prove that the neural network focuses on actual physical defect regions rather than background noise?

03 · Approach

Proposed Methodology

01

📥 Data Ingestion & Stratified Split

Raw BMP images loaded via dynamic OS path scanning into Pandas DataFrames. Pixel values normalized from [0,255] → [0.0,1.0]. All images resized to 224×224px to match MobileNetV2's ImageNet pre-training resolution. A stratified 70/15/15 split ensures equal class representation across Training (1,260), Validation (270), and Test (270) sets.

02

🔄 Real-Time Data Augmentation (Training Only)

To prevent overfitting on the small dataset, Keras ImageDataGenerator applies random augmentations during every training epoch: rotations (±20°), horizontal & vertical flips, width/height shifts (10%), zoom (±20%), and brightness shifts (80%–120%). Vertical flips are valid because steel defects have no semantic orientation.

03

🧠 Model 1: Custom CNN (4 Convolutional Blocks)

A sequential CNN built from scratch. Four Conv2D blocks with doubling filter sizes (32 → 64 → 128 → 256), each followed by Batch Normalization and MaxPooling(2×2). Classification head: Flatten → Dense(512, Dropout 0.5) → Dense(256, Dropout 0.5) → Softmax(6). Optimizer: Adam (lr=0.001). Callbacks: EarlyStopping, ReduceLROnPlateau, ModelCheckpoint.

04

🚀 Model 2: MobileNetV2 Transfer Learning (2-Phase)

Phase 1 — Feature Extraction: MobileNetV2 base weights fully frozen. Custom head (GlobalAvgPool → Dense 256 → Dropout 0.5 → Softmax) trained for 15 epochs at lr=0.001. Phase 2 — Fine-Tuning: All layers from index 100+ unfrozen. Trained at very low lr=0.00001 to adapt to steel textures without catastrophic forgetting of ImageNet weights.

05

📈 Evaluation, Metrics & Grad-CAM Explainability

Both models evaluated on the 270-image holdout Test Set using Accuracy, Weighted Precision, Recall, F1 Score, Confusion Matrix, and One-vs-Rest ROC Curves (AUC). Grad-CAM heatmaps generated on the final convolutional layer to visually verify the model localizes the actual physical defect regions correctly.

05 · Explainability

Grad-CAM Explainable AI

🔥 Opening the Black Box

Deep learning models are frequently criticized as "black boxes." Before deploying an AI system in a high-stakes manufacturing environment, it is critical to prove why the model makes its classification decisions.

We implemented Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping (Grad-CAM) using the gradients of the target class score flowing into the final convolutional layer to produce a spatial heatmap localization.

✅

Verified Result: The high-activation zones (deep red areas) on the Grad-CAM overlays physically map onto the exact location of the steel surface defects. The AI is genuinely acting as a computerized quality inspector — not relying on background noise.

🎯

Industrial Impact: This interpretability is essential for regulatory compliance and building factory operator trust in an AI-driven inspection system.

Real output from src/gradcam.py — Grad-CAM heatmaps across multiple defect classes. Red zones = highest neural network activation. Perfectly aligned with physical defects.

07 · Summary

Conclusion & Future Work

🎯 Key Findings

  • Transfer Learning is Superior: Fine-tuned MobileNetV2 achieved 98.15% accuracy and AUC=0.99, vastly outperforming Custom CNN's 92.59% on the 270-image holdout test set.
  • Custom CNN is Still Viable: The scratch-built model is lighter (17 MB vs 33 MB), faster (3.08 min vs 5.75 min), and achieves industrially acceptable 92.59% accuracy for resource-constrained deployments.
  • Grad-CAM Validates Trust: Heatmap overlays confirm models focus on actual physical defect anomalies, not background noise — critical for regulatory compliance.
  • Data Augmentation is Essential: Real-time augmentation (flips, rotations, zoom, brightness) was critical to prevent overfitting on the small 1,800-image dataset.
  • Stratified Split Ensures Fairness: The 70/15/15 stratified split guarantees equal class representation in training, validation, and holdout test sets.
